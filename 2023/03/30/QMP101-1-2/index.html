1. Coordinate transformation

Considering two different basises, \(\sum_{\alpha}|\alpha\rangle\langle\alpha | = 1\) and \(\sum_{\beta}|\beta\rangle\langle\beta | = 1\), then \[ |\alpha\rangle = \sum_{\beta}|\beta\rangle\langle\beta|\alpha\rangle \] If we view \(|\alpha\rangle\) as \(a_{\alpha}^{\dagger}|0\rangle\), i.e. we create the single-particle state \(|\alpha\rangle\) by a creation operator, then \[ a_{\alpha}^{\dagger}|0\rangle = \sum_{\beta}a_{\beta}^{\dagger}|0\rangle \langle\beta|\alpha\rangle \] or \[ \begin{split} a_{\alpha}^{\dagger} = \sum_{\beta}\langle\beta|\alpha\rangle a_{\beta}^{\dagger} \\ a_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta}\langle\alpha|\beta\rangle a_{\beta} \end{split} \] Specifically, if \(\beta \equiv \vec{r}\) we have \[ \begin{split} & a_{\alpha} = \int d\vec{r}\ \langle \alpha|\vec{r}\rangle \hat{\psi}(\vec{r}) = \int d\vec{r}\ \psi_{\alpha}^{*}(\vec{r}) \hat{\psi}(\vec{r}) \\ & \hat{\psi}(\vec{r}) = \sum_{\alpha}\langle \vec{r}|\alpha\rangle a_{\alpha} = \sum_{\alpha} \psi_{\alpha} (\vec{r}) a_{\alpha} \end{split} \] If \(\alpha \equiv \vec{k}\), then \[ \langle \vec{r}|\vec{k} \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}}e^{i\vec{k}\cdot \vec{r}} \] and \[ \hat{\psi}(\vec{r}) = \sum_{\vec{k}}\langle \vec{r}|\vec{k}\rangle a_{\vec{k}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{V}}\sum_{\vec{k}} a_{\vec{k}} e^{i\vec{k}\cdot \vec{r}} \]

where \(V\) is some normalized factor.

2. Single-body operator

We only consider bosons here. The discussion for fermions is similar (pay attention to the sign when exchanging two fermionic operators) and will lead to exactly the same form with the bosons'.

Suppose we have \(N\) particles, and \(N\) single-body operators \(\hat{h}\), whose spectrum expansion is \[ \hat{h} = \sum_{\alpha} \lambda_{\alpha}|\alpha\rangle\langle\alpha| \] we expect \(\hat{H} = \sum_{i = 1}^ N\hat{h}_i = \sum_{\alpha}\lambda_{\alpha} \hat{n}_{\alpha}\) because the expected value \[ \langle \{n_{\alpha}\} | \hat{H} | \{n_{\alpha} \} \rangle = \sum_{\alpha} \lambda_{\alpha} n_{\alpha} \] is exactly what we want.

Then \[ \begin{split} \hat{H} =& \sum_{\alpha} \lambda_{\alpha} a_{\alpha}^{\dagger} a_{\alpha} \\ = & \sum_{\alpha} \langle \alpha|\hat{h}|\alpha\rangle a_{\alpha}^{\dagger} a_{\alpha} \\ = & \sum_{\alpha\beta}a_{\alpha}^{\dagger} \langle \alpha |\hat{h} |\beta\rangle a_{\beta} \end{split} \] or \[ \hat{H} = \sum_{\alpha\beta}a_{\alpha}^{\dagger} \langle \alpha |\hat{h} |\beta\rangle a_{\beta},\qquad [‚ñ≤] \] [‚ñ≤] is a general form and is independent of the choice of basis.

To prove that, considering another basis \(\sum_{\gamma}|\gamma\rangle \langle\gamma|\), then we find \[ \hat{H} = \sum_{\alpha\beta\gamma} a_{\alpha}^{\dagger}\langle {\alpha} |\gamma\rangle \langle \gamma |\hat{h} |\beta\rangle a_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta\gamma} a_{\gamma}^{\dagger}\langle \gamma |\hat{h}|\beta\rangle a_{\beta} \] If \(\alpha=\vec{r}\) and \(\beta=\vec{r}'\) in [‚ñ≤], we get \[ \hat{H} = \int d\vec{r}d\vec{r}'\ \hat{\psi}^{\dagger}(\vec{r}) \langle \vec{r} | \hat{h}|\vec{r}'\rangle\hat{\psi}(\vec{r}') \] Specifically, if \(\hat{h} = V(\vec{r})\), then \[ \hat{H} = \int d\vec{r}\ \hat{\psi}^{\dagger}(\vec{r}) V(\vec{r})\hat{\psi}(\vec{r}) \]

3. Two-body operator

We similarly consider the two-body operator \(\hat{h}^2\), whose specturm expansion is \[ \hat{h}^2 = \sum_{\alpha\beta} |\alpha\rangle|\beta\rangle\langle\alpha|\langle\beta|\lambda_{\alpha\beta} \]

Note that not all \(\hat{h}^2\) can be expressed as above, but it is sufficient to consider this form within our discussions.

For \(N\) particles, we have \[ \begin{split} \hat{H}^2 = & \sum_{\alpha<\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} \hat{n}_{\alpha}\hat{n}_{\beta} + \sum_{\alpha=\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} \frac{\hat{n}_{\alpha}(\hat{n}_{\alpha}-1)}{2} \\ = & \frac{1}{2} \bigg[\sum_{\alpha\ne\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} \hat{n}_{\alpha}\hat{n}_{\beta} + \sum_{\alpha=\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} {\hat{n}_{\alpha}(\hat{n}_{\alpha}-1)}\bigg] \\ = & \frac{1}{2} \bigg[\sum_{\alpha\ne\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} a^{\dagger}_{\alpha}a_{\alpha} a^{\dagger}_{\beta}a_{\beta} + \sum_{\alpha=\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} a^{\dagger}_{\alpha}a_{\alpha}(a^{\dagger}_{\alpha}a_{\alpha}-1) \bigg] \\ = & \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha\beta} \lambda_{\alpha\beta} a^{\dagger}_{\alpha}a^{\dagger}_{\beta}a_{\alpha} a_{\beta} \\ = & \hat{H}^2 = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\alpha\beta}a_{\alpha}^{\dagger}a_{\beta}^{\dagger}\langle \alpha\beta|\hat{h}^2|\alpha\beta\rangle a_{\beta}a_{\alpha} \end{split} \] where we use the relation \([a_{\alpha},a_{\alpha}^{\dagger}]=1\).

We can similarly prove that \[ \hat{H}^2 = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta}a_{\alpha}^{\dagger}a_{\beta}^{\dagger}\langle \alpha\beta|\hat{h}^2|\gamma\delta\rangle a_{\delta}a_{\gamma} \] is independent of the choice of basis.

Specifically, if \(\hat{h}^2\) is the Coloumb potential, i.e. \[ \hat{h}^2 = U(\vec{r}-\vec{r}') = \frac{e^2}{|\vec{r} - \vec{r}'|} \] We have \[ \hat{H}^2 = \frac{1}{2}\int d\vec{r}d\vec{r}'\ \hat{\psi}^{\dagger}(\vec{r})\hat{\psi}^{\dagger}(\vec{r}') \frac{e^2}{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}'|} \hat{\psi}(\vec{r'}) \hat{\psi}(\vec{r}) \]
